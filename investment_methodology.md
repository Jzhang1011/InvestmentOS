# Investment Methodology

## Purpose

Determine whether reality has changed enough to update an investment thesis.

Markets move daily. Structural reality usually changes more slowly.

## Core Principles

- Monitor assumptions, not headlines.
- Evaluate causes before outcomes.
- Prefer structural evidence over short-term events.
- Price movement alone never validates a thesis.
- Every conclusion must be supported by evidence.
- Explain why a development matters.
- A useful alert is more valuable than frequent alerts.

## Evidence Classification

### Fact

Objectively verifiable information.

### Interpretation

A reasoned conclusion supported by facts.

### Speculation

A possible outcome without sufficient confirmation.

### Unknown

Insufficient evidence. Do not guess.

## Review Process

### 1. Collect new facts

Focus on policy decisions, economic releases, capital flows, filings, and structural developments.

### 2. Test relevance

Ask which thesis assumption, if any, the fact affects.

Classify impact:

- None
- Minor
- Moderate
- Material

### 3. Test durability

Ask whether the development is temporary or likely to change long-term expectations.

### 4. Determine direction

- Supports thesis
- Weakens thesis
- Contradicts thesis
- No meaningful effect

### 5. Update thesis health

Change the score only when underlying assumptions change.

### 6. Examine interactions

One indicator rarely changes a thesis. Multiple aligned indicators deserve escalation.

### 7. Identify second-order effects

Trace:

```text
Evidence → Assumption → Thesis → Sector → Company → Portfolio
```

## Thesis Health Guide

- **90–100:** Exceptional evidence; few conflicting signals.
- **75–89:** Strong evidence; manageable uncertainties.
- **60–74:** Reasonable support; balanced risks.
- **40–59:** Conflicting evidence; thesis needs reassessment.
- **Below 40:** Weak or invalid thesis.

Thesis health is not the probability of investment profit.

## Escalation

- No assumptions changed → no portfolio change.
- One assumption changed → increase monitoring.
- Two assumptions changed → reassess thesis.
- Three or more changed → review portfolio exposure.

## Common Errors

- Confusing price with evidence.
- Confusing narrative with reality.
- Extrapolating recent events indefinitely.
- Ignoring contrary evidence.
- Assigning false precision to uncertain forecasts.
- Recommending a company based only on macro exposure.
